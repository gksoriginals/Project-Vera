## 1. Workshop Overview

### Overall Plan

1. Co-design with the deaf community: Conduct two workshops to gather insights, address pain points, and refine prototypes based on prioritized feedback.
2. Beta development and iteration: Develop the initial beta version, incorporate low-hanging-fruit feedback, and release Beta 2 after the second workshop.
3. Expert validation: Engage accessibility experts in the final co-design session to ensure inclusivity and usability.
4. Final refinement and industry pilots: Deliver a validated and refined version of vera AI, ready for real-world implementation.

Note from the source: dates and times for the second co-design workshop were to be provided after analyzing the first event and determining the timeline for feature improvements required for the pilot.

### Workshop Objectives

1. Identifying pain points in overloaded captions.
2. Prioritising essential features of inclusive captions using the MoSCoW method.
3. Love, Break and Fix: testing and feedback of vera AI.

### Initial Participant Plan

The planning document proposed the following participant profile:

1. 6 individuals aged 20 to 35.
2. Deaf users across the spectrum of deafness, including:
   - Higher hearing loss, oral communication, lipreading deaf person.
   - Moderate hearing loss, oral communication with clarity, less lipreading or no lipreading at all.
   - Deaf persons who do not practice oral communication and prefer sign language as their mode of communication.
   - Deaf students.
   - Deaf employees.
3. Participants who had used live and real-time captioning at least once.
4. Participants with basic English language understanding.

- Candidate search for the first session: March 31, 2025
- First co-design workshop: April 12, 2025
- Classroom and workplace co-design workshop: April 19 and 20, 2025
- Co-design workshop with AI technologists and business experts: May 3

## 2. Agenda and Facilitation Plan

### Activity 1: Icebreaker Session (15 mins)

1. Introduction to `people+ai`.
2. Participant introductions in a fun format.
3. Introduction of the vera team and explanation of the 4Ws.

### Activity 2: Identifying Pain Points (20 mins)

1. Present a live or recorded session with unfiltered captions. Participants observe and identify pain points.
2. Each participant writes down one issue per sticky note.
3. Place the sticky notes on a shared board or wall.
4. Facilitate a guided discussion where participants group similar issues.
5. Summarise the key pain points.

Expected result from the plan: a structured understanding of the primary challenges users face with overloaded captions.

### Activity 3: Prioritising Features with the MoSCoW Method (45 mins)

1. Introduce potential features and enhancements such as filler-word reduction, speaker identification, keyword highlighting, and adjustable speed.
2. Ask participants to prioritise features using the MoSCoW method:
   - Must Have: essential for accessibility and usability.
   - Should Have: important but not critical.
   - Could Have: nice to have but not urgent.
   - Won't Have: not necessary at this stage.
3. Facilitate a discussion where participants weigh pros and cons across tensions such as:
   - Accuracy vs. simplicity
   - Automation vs. user control
   - Design complexity vs. accessibility
4. Refine the prioritisation list based on discussion and real-world considerations.

### Activity 4: Testing and Iteration (20 mins)

Purpose: validate refinements and further improve the captioning experience.

1. Present a prototype of vera AI's improved live captions, incorporating all the features. Participants engage with the demo or prototype in a real-time environment.
2. Gather feedback regarding clarity, timing, readability, and overall usability.
3. Facilitate a discussion to identify any remaining gaps, unintended issues, or additional improvements.
4. Summarise key takeaways and define action points for further iteration and refinement.

#### Feedback Questions Listed in the Planning Material

1. Can participants quickly understand the responses?
2. What's confusing here?
3. What ideas should we move forward with?
4. Should we use AI to decide what to simplify and what to highlight?
5. Should we place buttons for manually choosing simplification or highlighting?
6. Do you think you can confidently communicate with vera TTS?
7. Where do you see vera TTS being more useful?
8. In-person or online: where do you see vera being more useful?

The planning notes also state that instead of leading with questions like whether AI should decide simplification or highlighting, facilitators should first clear participants' doubts and then use the above as follow-up questions.

### Expected Outcomes from the Planning Material

- Clear insights into the limitations of existing captioning and helper tools used by deaf users in various real-life settings in India.
- Actionable, user-driven design improvements for vera AI.
- A prioritised feature list to guide future vera AI development.
- Documented outcomes that can be converted into insights for potential stakeholders.

## 3. Research Summary from the Workshop Report

### Title and Project Framing

**Designing for Inclusive Communication: Insights from a Co-Design Workshop on Live Captioning Systems**

- Project: vera AI
- Facilitators: Gopi and Divya Prabha

### Introduction

The report states that live captioning systems are often designed around assumptions of fluent, standardised speech and monolingual language use. This leaves out the diverse communication needs of:

- Deaf and hard-of-hearing people
- People with speech irregularities
- Users who rely on mixed-language communication

The co-design workshop was used to surface lived experiences and identify challenges. These insights informed the development of vera AI as a live-captioning prototype intended to be inclusive and adaptive.

### High-Level Findings

- Validation of existing features: all current vera AI features were confirmed to be valuable.
- Identified improvements: caption speed control, mixed-language caption support, and adaptable TTS tone or style based on context were recognized as needed improvements.
- Additional feature requests: multiple speaker identification and accent detection were requested.
- Workshop repetition deemed unnecessary: the report says the insights from the co-design workshops were sufficient and additional workshop iterations were not needed.
- Broader impact: vera AI was seen as useful not only in academic and professional settings, but also in day-to-day communication tasks.

### Next Steps from the Report

1. Engage potential adopters in workplace settings and assess market viability.
2. Improve existing MVP features based on participant feedback.
3. Conduct a feasibility study with tech experts to validate additional feature requests and identify key features for implementation.
4. Benchmark current capabilities and evaluate privacy, bias, and ethical considerations.
5. Run pilots of vera AI with selected adopters.

## 4. Participants and Methodology

### Participants

The report describes seven participants aged 20 to 35 representing the Deaf and hard-of-hearing community and people with speech irregularities.

It notes variation in:

- Hearing conditions, from partial to profound hearing loss
- English language fluency
- Communication modes, including Indian Sign Language (ISL), lip reading, oral speech, and written text
- Professional and linguistic backgrounds
- Student and professional contexts

The report explicitly lists the following hearing and communication profiles:

1. Deaf persons who do not practice oral communication and prefer sign language as mode of communication.
2. Higher hearing loss, oral communication, lipreading deaf.
3. Deaf, limited lipreading, better communication with a hearing aid, and good communication using sign language.
4. Deaf persons who do not practice oral communication and prefer sign language as mode of communication.
5. Moderate hearing loss, oral communication with clarity, less lip-reading or no lip-reading at all.

### Methodology

- Format: in-person workshop
- Duration: 3 hours
- Documentation methods: field notes, discussion, group synthesis activities, and participant artefacts

The workshop was structured into three sequential co-design activities:

1. Mapping pain points in current live captioning systems
2. Prioritising feature enhancements using the MoSCoW method
3. Evaluating the vera AI prototype using Love, Break, and Fix

## 5. Activity 1: Pain Points in Current Live Captioning Systems

The purpose of this activity was to surface the nuanced realities and failures of current live-captioning technologies as experienced by Deaf, hard-of-hearing, and speech-irregularity users.

The report says the raw data was collected using sticky notes and then subjected to thematic analysis.

### Thematic Analysis Results

| Theme | Description |
| --- | --- |
| Non-standard speed of live captions | Users lacked control over the speed and flow of captions. Rapid text made it difficult to keep up or return to missed information. |
| Background noise | Irrelevant audio such as filler words and background sound was transcribed, cluttering captions with non-essential content. |
| Speaker identity | The absence of speaker labels led to confusion, especially in multi-person conversations. |
| Multilingual problems | Captions failed to handle multilingual utterances or translate across languages, privileging monolingual English speakers. |
| Accent bias | Regional, global, or non-native English accents were misinterpreted or corrected inaccurately, reflecting system bias. |
| Language understanding challenges | Users had difficulty following advanced or non-conversational English in captioned form, especially when cognitively overwhelmed. |
| Context awareness problems | Captions lacked situational awareness and failed to reflect speaker emotion, tone, emphasis, or interactional cues. |
| Responding problems | Live caption apps help deaf people to listen, but responding is still a challenge. |
| Accuracy vs. misleading meaning | Captions might be technically accurate but still misrepresent meaning or intention, making them misleading or emotionally distancing. |
| Misinterpretation | Systems made semantic or phonetic errors, such as turning speech into the wrong word or misunderstanding context entirely. |
| Cognitive dissonance | It is hard to switch attention between captions and the surroundings. |
| System and app accessibility | Live captions and text-to-speech accessibility are not available in many systems and apps. |

### Insight from Activity 1

The report highlights multilingual difficulties, language comprehension issues, cognitive dissonance, and challenges in responding as especially prominent concerns. It explicitly notes that these pain points align with the core issues vera aims to address.

## 6. Activity 2: Feature Prioritisation Using MoSCoW

To explore possible product improvements, the team ran a participatory prioritisation exercise based on the problems identified in Activity 1.

The report says:

- Participants were introduced to feature prompts generated using ChatGPT plus the team's AI expertise.
- The prompts were intended to address the major pain points.
- Participants prioritised them using the MoSCoW method:
  - Must Have: essential for accessibility and usability
  - Should Have: important but not critical
  - Could Have: nice to have but not urgent
  - Won't Have: unnecessary or potentially distracting

### Prioritised Features and Descriptions

| Prioritized feature | Description | Number of people requested |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Elaborate points on demand | Click words and phrases in live captions to read the meanings. | 3 |

### Insight from Activity 2

The MoSCoW activity affirmed that all features currently in vera are prioritized by users. The team planned to assess the remaining requested features for technical feasibility and ease of implementation, then prioritize them accordingly.

## 7. Activity 3: Love, Break, Fix on the vera AI Prototype

This activity was used to gather actionable feedback by exploring what participants valued, what might break the experience, and how the prototype could be improved.

The report states that participants engaged with the demo or prototype of vera AI's live captions, including both text-to-speech and speech-to-text functionality.

### Love / Break / Fix Notes Captured in the Report

| Love / Strengths | Break / Issues | Fix / Improvement Direction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| LLM fixing input text before passing to text-to-speech | UI/UX needs to be improved to help deaf individuals understand how to use the app | Improve clarity of the interface and onboarding |
| Text highlighting | LLM should provide multiple possible text options instead of automatically fixing text inputs that go to TTS | Offer multiple rewrite or correction choices |
| AI automatically switches between simplification and highlighting | Tone and style of TTS output should change based on professional versus casual settings | Make TTS output context-sensitive |
| Text-to-speech | Need real-time text correction | Add real-time text correction support |

### Insight from Activity 3

Participants found all features in the current version of vera AI helpful. The activity helped identify both the successful aspects of the prototype and the areas that still required refinement.

## 8. Consolidated Insights Across Both Source Documents

Taken together, the two documents point to the following combined picture:

1. Current captioning tools create significant overload through speed, noise, poor speaker separation, and inaccurate handling of multilingual and accented speech.
2. Inclusive captioning is not only about transcription accuracy. It also depends on readability, timing control, contextual awareness, language accessibility, and support for response generation.
3. vera's current feature set already maps well to user priorities.
4. The strongest improvement opportunities are:
   - Adjustable caption speed
   - Mixed-language support
   - Multi-speaker identification
   - Better TTS voice quality and contextual tone
   - On-demand explanation or elaboration of difficult words and phrases
   - Stronger UI clarity and better control over AI-mediated rewriting
5. The product appears relevant across classrooms, workplaces, and everyday communication scenarios.
6. The workshop outputs were considered strong enough to move into feasibility validation, benchmarking, and pilot preparation rather than continuing to run repeated co-design workshops.
